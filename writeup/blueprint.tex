\section*{Status}
This blueprint is not complete yet. The abstract SOCP component has been
formalized, and orthogonal mixing of the four factor coordinates is now also
formalized, but the ternary-quartic algebraic reduction is still missing.

\section*{Formalized Step}
Let $r(u,p) = \sigma(u) - p$. The following statement is already formalized in
\texttt{TernaryQuarticProof/Certificate.lean}.

\medskip
\noindent
\textbf{Lemma.} Assume:
\begin{enumerate}
\item $u$ is an admissible rank-4 point;
\item $u$ is a SOCP for $p$ with respect to a positive-definite bilinear form
$B$;
\item $r(u,p)$ is orthogonal to $\operatorname{im}(A_{u_{\mathrm{img}}})$ for
some admissible factor $u_{\mathrm{img}}$;
\item there is a decomposition
\[
  p = q_{\mathrm{img}} + \sum_{w \in W} \sigma(w),
\]
where $q_{\mathrm{img}} \in \operatorname{im}(A_{u_{\mathrm{img}}})$ and every
$w \in W$ is an admissible kernel direction for $A_u$.
\end{enumerate}
Then $r(u,p) = 0$.

\medskip
\noindent
\textbf{Proof.}
The image term pairs to zero by the first-order condition. Each kernel-square
term pairs nonnegatively with the residual by the second-order condition,
because $A_u(w)=0$ removes the positive $2\langle A_u(w), A_u(w)\rangle$ part
of the Hessian expression. Summing these inequalities gives
$\langle p, r(u,p)\rangle \ge 0$. On the other hand, the first-order condition
at $v=u$ gives $\langle \sigma(u), r(u,p)\rangle = 0$, hence
\[
  \langle r(u,p), r(u,p)\rangle
  = \langle \sigma(u)-p, r(u,p)\rangle
  = - \langle p, r(u,p)\rangle \le 0.
\]
Positive-definiteness implies $\langle r(u,p), r(u,p)\rangle \ge 0$, so the
objective is zero and therefore $r(u,p)=0$.

\section*{Additional Formalized Reductions}
Two special cases are now formalized.

\medskip
\noindent
\textbf{Image case.} If $p \in \operatorname{im}(A_u)$ with an admissible
preimage, then $r(u,p)=0$ directly from the first-order condition and positive
definiteness.

\medskip
\noindent
\textbf{Constant-relation case.} If there exists a nonzero constant vector
$c \in \mathbb{R}^4$ with $\sum_i c_i u_i = 0$, then $r(u,p)=0$ for every SOS
quartic $p$.

\medskip
\noindent
\textbf{Proof sketch.}
For each quadratic summand $q^2$ in an SOS decomposition of $p$, define the
admissible direction $w_i = c_i q$. Then
\[
  A_u(w) = q \sum_i c_i u_i = 0,
  \qquad
  \sigma(w) = \Bigl(\sum_i c_i^2\Bigr) q^2.
\]
Since $\sum_i c_i^2 > 0$, the SOCP inequality in the direction $w$ implies
$\langle q^2, r(u,p)\rangle \ge 0$. Summing over the SOS decomposition yields
$\langle p, r(u,p)\rangle \ge 0$, and the abstract SOCP argument finishes as
before.

\medskip
\noindent
\textbf{Orthogonal factor-coordinate invariance.} Let $M \in \mathbb{R}^{4
\times 4}$ satisfy $M^\top M = M M^\top = I$, and define
\[
  (\operatorname{mix}_M u)_i = \sum_j M_{ij} u_j.
\]
Then the following are now formalized in
\texttt{TernaryQuarticProof/FactorTransform.lean}:
\begin{enumerate}
\item admissibility is preserved by $\operatorname{mix}_M$;
\item $A_{\operatorname{mix}_M u}(v) = A_u(\operatorname{mix}_{M^\top} v)$;
\item $\sigma(\operatorname{mix}_M u) = \sigma(u)$ and hence
$r(p,\operatorname{mix}_M u)=r(p,u)$;
\item FOCP and SOCP are preserved under orthogonal mixing.
\end{enumerate}
Therefore the eventual factor-space case analysis may be carried out up to
orthogonal recombination of the four coordinates.

\medskip
\noindent
\textbf{Formalized representative surjectivity.} The constant
$\dim(W \cap \mathbb{R}[x_1,x_2]_{\le 1}) = 1$ model
\[
  u_{\mathrm{const}} = (1, x_1^2, x_1x_2, x_2^2)
\]
is now fully formalized in
\texttt{TernaryQuarticProof/RepresentativeSurjective.lean}. The key lemma is
that every quartic monomial of degree at most $4$ lies in
$\operatorname{im}(A_{u_{\mathrm{const}}})$, hence every quartic polynomial does
as well by linearity. Therefore every SOCP at this representative has zero
residual by the already-formalized image case.

\section*{Normal-Form Target}
The most promising unresolved case is when, after affine changes of variables
and invertible recombination of the four coordinates, the factor takes the form
\[
  u = (1, x_1, x_2, q).
\]
For this normal form there is an explicit admissible kernel family:
if a homogeneous quadratic $h$ is written as
\[
  h = x_1 a + x_2 b
\]
with $a,b$ linear, then
\[
  w = (-h, a, b, 0)
\]
satisfies $A_u(w)=0$ and
\[
  \sigma(w) = h^2 + a^2 + b^2.
\]
The correction term $a^2+b^2$ has degree at most $2$, hence lies in the image
through the first coordinate $1$. Therefore every square of a homogeneous
quadratic belongs to
\[
  \operatorname{im}(A_u) + \operatorname{cone}(\sigma(\ker A_u)).
\]
The remaining gap is to turn this observation into a complete classification of
all independent rank-$4$ factors of image rank $13$ or $14$.

\section*{Correction To The Previous Case Split}
The earlier five-case list is not complete. The constant-containing
$\dim(W \cap \mathbb{R}[x_1,x_2]_{\le 1}) = 2$ branch splits further.

\medskip
\noindent
Representative mixed-affine models recorded in the Julia script are:
\[
  (1,x_1,x_1^2,x_1x_2),\qquad
  (1,x_1,x_1x_2,x_2^2),\qquad
  (1,x_1,x_1^2,x_2^2).
\]
Their image ranks are respectively $13$, $14$, and $15$, with missing quotient
monomials
\[
  \{x_2^3,x_2^4\},\qquad \{x_1^4\},\qquad \varnothing.
\]
Moreover the first two mixed-affine representatives also admit explicit kernel
directions:
\[
  u=(1,x_1,x_1^2,x_1x_2),\qquad
  w_\ell=(0,0,-x_2\ell,x_1\ell),\qquad \ell=c+d x_2,
\]
for which
\[
  A_u(w_\ell)=0,\qquad
  \sigma(w_\ell)=(x_1^2+x_2^2)\ell^2 \equiv x_2^2\ell^2
  \equiv 2cd\,x_2^3 + d^2 x_2^4
  \pmod{\operatorname{im}(A_u)}.
\]
Hence this family generates the two missing quotient classes. For
\[
  u=(1,x_1,x_1x_2,x_2^2),
\]
the direction
\[
  w=(0,0,-x_1x_2,x_1^2)
\]
satisfies
\[
  A_u(w)=0,\qquad \sigma(w)=x_1^2x_2^2+x_1^4 \equiv x_1^4
  \pmod{\operatorname{im}(A_u)}.
\]
So the mixed-affine algebra itself now looks tractable; the old statement that
the proof was complete up to normalization was too optimistic because the
classification step still has to produce these representatives.

\medskip
\noindent
The current candidate normalization for the constant-containing
$\dim = 2$ branch is the following. After choosing coordinates so that
\[
  W \cap \mathbb{R}[x_1,x_2]_{\le 1} = \operatorname{span}(1,x_1),
\]
the remaining quadratic plane is a $2$-dimensional subspace of
\[
  \operatorname{span}(x_1^2,x_1x_2,x_2^2),
\]
hence the kernel of a nonzero binary quadratic
\[
  q = a x_1^2 + b x_1x_2 + c x_2^2
\]
in the dual $3$-space. Under shears and rescalings preserving the line
$\mathbb{R}x_1$, the quadratic $q$ should reduce to one of
\[
  x_2^2,\qquad x_1^2,\qquad x_1x_2,\qquad x_1^2 \pm x_2^2.
\]
The Julia probes show that the last three possibilities are already
surjective/image-rank-$15$ except for the $x_1^2$ case, which gives image rank
$14$, while the $x_2^2$ case gives image rank $13$. So the only nonsurjective
mixed-affine representatives appear to be
\[
  (1,x_1,x_1^2,x_1x_2),\qquad (1,x_1,x_1x_2,x_2^2).
\]
Proving this reduction is now the key written sublemma.

\section*{Current Candidate Blueprint}
At the level of a paper proof, the present candidate argument is:

\begin{enumerate}
\item If the four coordinates of $u$ are linearly dependent over $\mathbb{R}$,
apply the already-formalized constant-relation lemma.
\item Otherwise let $W = \operatorname{span}(u_1,u_2,u_3,u_4)$ and classify by
the dimension of $W \cap \mathbb{R}[x_1,x_2]_{\le 1}$.
\item Use orthogonal changes in the factor coordinates together with affine
changes of variables in $(x_1,x_2)$ to reduce the stable branches to the
following models:
  \[
    (1,x_1,x_2,h),\quad
    (x_1,x_2,x_1^2,x_1x_2),\quad
    (x_1,x_2,x_1^2,x_2^2),\quad
    (1,x_1^2,x_1x_2,x_2^2),\quad
    (x_1,x_1^2,x_1x_2,x_2^2).
  \]
\item In these stable models, show directly that every quadratic square lies in
  \[
    \operatorname{im}(A_u) + \operatorname{cone}(\sigma(\ker A_u))
  \]
  using explicit kernel families:
  \begin{itemize}
  \item $(1,x_1,x_2,h)$:
    \[
      w=(-h_0,a,b,0),\qquad h_0=x_1a+x_2b;
    \]
  \item $(x_1,x_2,x_1^2,x_1x_2)$:
    \[
      w_c=(-2x_2,x_1,0,1),\qquad
      w_q=(-x_2^2,x_1x_2,0,0);
    \]
  \item $(x_1,x_2,x_1^2,x_2^2)$:
    \[
      w=(-x_1,0,1,0);
    \]
  \item $(1,x_1^2,x_1x_2,x_2^2)$: surjective image, no kernel term needed;
  \item $(x_1,x_1^2,x_1x_2,x_2^2)$:
    \[
      w_\ell=(-x_2\ell,0,\ell,0),\qquad \ell=c+d x_2.
    \]
  \end{itemize}
\item The model
  \[
    (1,x_1^2,x_1x_2,x_2^2)
  \]
  is already formalized: its image is all quartics.
\item Treat the mixed-affine branch
  \[
    (1,x_1,x_1^2,x_1x_2),\qquad
    (1,x_1,x_1x_2,x_2^2),\qquad
    (1,x_1,x_1^2,x_2^2)
  \]
  separately. The representative quotient generators are now explicit:
  the first model uses the family
  \[
    w_\ell=(0,0,-x_2\ell,x_1\ell),\qquad \ell=c+d x_2,
  \]
  the second uses
  \[
    w=(0,0,-x_1x_2,x_1^2),
  \]
  and the third is already surjective. What is still missing is the written
  proof that every constant-containing $\dim = 2$ factor can be normalized into
  one of these representatives.
\item Apply the formalized admissible image-plus-cone certificate to conclude
$\sigma(u)=p$.
\end{enumerate}

\noindent
The main obstacle to a finished Lean proof is now precise: complete the
normalization/classification of the mixed-affine constant-containing
$\dim = 2$ branch, then translate the representative kernel formulas above into
Lean and assemble the final theorem.

\section*{Pending Step}
To finish the theorem, it remains to prove the multivariate admissible
image-plus-cone decomposition for every SOS quartic. This is currently the main
mathematical gap. The likely form is a finite-dimensional quadratic-space
certificate rather than a direct copy of the univariate gcd/peeling argument.
