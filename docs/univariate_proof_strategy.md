This document summarizes the proof that the rank-2 B-M factorization of the SOS
decomposition problem has no spurious second-order critical points (SOCPs).  It
is written in general terms to guide potential extensions to multivariate
polynomials.

# Setup and Objective

The Burer–Monteiro factorization replaces the PSD variable $X \succeq 0$ in the
SOS-SDP with $X = UU^\top$, giving the nonconvex quadratic-penalty objective

$$f_p(\mathbf{u}) = \|\sigma(\mathbf{u}) - p\|^2, \quad
  \sigma(\mathbf{u}) = \textstyle\sum_{i=1}^r u_i^2.$$

The key linear map associated with $\mathbf{u}$ is

$$\mathcal{A}_\mathbf{u} : \mathbf{v} \mapsto \textstyle\sum_{i=1}^r u_i v_i.$$

Note that $\mathcal{A}_\mathbf{u}(\mathbf{u}) = \sigma(\mathbf{u})$ and
$\sigma(\mathbf{v}) \in \operatorname{cone}(\sigma(\ker \mathcal{A}_\mathbf{u}))$
whenever $\mathbf{v} \in \ker \mathcal{A}_\mathbf{u}$.

# First- and Second-Order Necessary Conditions

A point $\mathbf{u}$ is a **SOCP** if for all $\mathbf{v}$:

- **(Gradient)** $\langle \mathcal{A}_\mathbf{u}(\mathbf{v}),\; \sigma(\mathbf{u}) - p \rangle = 0$
- **(Hessian)** $\langle \sigma(\mathbf{v}),\; \sigma(\mathbf{u}) - p \rangle + 2\|\mathcal{A}_\mathbf{u}(\mathbf{v})\|^2 \ge 0$

The gradient condition says $\sigma(\mathbf{u}) - p \perp \operatorname{im}(\mathcal{A}_\mathbf{u})$;
the Hessian condition links the residual to $\operatorname{cone}(\sigma(\ker \mathcal{A}_\mathbf{u}))$.

# Canonical Decomposition of $\mathbf{u}$

For $r = 2$ and $\mathbf{u} = (u_1, u_2)$, write
$u_i = u_i' g h$ where

| Factor | Property |
|--------|----------|
| $u_1', u_2'$ | coprime: $\gcd(u_1', u_2') = 1$ |
| $g$ | $\gcd(u_1, u_2)$ part coprime with $\sigma(\mathbf{u}') = {u_1'}^2 + {u_2'}^2$ |
| $h$ | remaining part of $\gcd(u_1,u_2)$; every root of $h$ is also a root of $\sigma(\mathbf{u}')$ |

Key structural facts: $\sigma(\mathbf{u}')$ has **no real roots** (since $u_1', u_2'$
are coprime), and therefore $h$ also has no real roots and even degree.

# Case-Based Analysis: Three Sufficient Conditions

The proof proceeds through three increasingly general sufficient conditions on
$p$ (relative to $\mathbf{u}$), each implying $f_p(\mathbf{u}) = 0$ at any SOCP.

## C1 — Coprime case ($g = h = 1$)

**Condition**: $p \in \operatorname{im}(\mathcal{A}_\mathbf{u})$.

**Argument**: By Bézout's identity (image surjectivity when $u_1', u_2'$ are
coprime), there exists $\mathbf{v}$ with $\mathcal{A}_\mathbf{u}(\mathbf{v}) =
\sigma(\mathbf{u}) - p$.  Plugging into the gradient condition gives
$f_p(\mathbf{u}) = \|\sigma(\mathbf{u})-p\|^2 = 0$.

**Generalization note**: Requires a surjectivity result analogous to Bézout for
the multivariate map $\mathcal{A}_\mathbf{u}$.  In the multivariate setting this
corresponds to the ideal generated by $u_1, \ldots, u_r$ covering the ambient
polynomial space (no common base locus).

## C2 — Shared factor coprime with $\sigma(\mathbf{u}')$ ($h = 1$)

**Condition**: $p \in \operatorname{im}(\mathcal{A}_\mathbf{u}) + \operatorname{cone}(\sigma(\ker \mathcal{A}_\mathbf{u}))$.

**Argument**: Decompose $p = \mathcal{A}_\mathbf{u}(\mathbf{v}) + \sum_i \sigma(\mathbf{w}^{(i)})$
with $\mathbf{w}^{(i)} \in \ker \mathcal{A}_\mathbf{u}$.  The gradient condition
kills the first term; the Hessian condition gives $\langle \sigma(\mathbf{w}^{(i)}),
\sigma(\mathbf{u}) - p \rangle \ge 0$.  Combining with
$\langle \sigma(\mathbf{u}), \sigma(\mathbf{u}) - p \rangle = 0$ forces
$f_p(\mathbf{u}) \le 0$, and nonnegativity gives $f_p = 0$.

**Main algebraic lemma (hgroup)**: To establish that every SOS $p$ satisfies C2
when $h = 1$, one needs: given coprime $g$ and SOS $q$, there exists SOS $s$
with $p \equiv sq \pmod{g}$.  The proof uses:
1. **Bézout mod $g$**: find $a$ with $aq \equiv 1 \pmod{g}$ (from coprimality);
2. **SOS square root mod $g$**: since $a > 0$ on real roots of $g$, use Hermite
   interpolation (Taylor series of $\sqrt{a}$ around each root of $g$, glued by
   CRT) to find $t$ with $a \equiv t^2 \pmod{g}$;
3. Then $p \equiv t^2 p q \pmod{g}$, giving the required SOS $s$.

**Generalization note**: The hgroup lemma needs a multivariate analogue.  The
critical question is whether $p \equiv sq \pmod{I}$ for an ideal $I$ generated
by $g_1, \ldots$ has a SOS solution $s$.  This likely requires tools from
algebraic geometry (e.g., Nullstellensatz, Positivstellensatz on varieties).

## C3 — General case (arbitrary $g, h$)

**Condition**: Replace $h$ by $x_1^k$ (a product of real linear factors) in C2:
$p \in \operatorname{im}(\mathcal{A}_{\mathbf{u} x_1^k/h}) + \operatorname{cone}(\sigma(\ker \mathcal{A}_\mathbf{u}))$.

**Argument**: The roots of $h$ are complex; the key insight is to "replace" each
complex quadratic factor $r_i \mid h$ with real factor $x_1^2$ iteratively.
For each factor $r_i$, one shows that $r_i \mid \sigma(\mathbf{u}')$ and
$r_i \mid \gcd(u_1,u_2)$ imply that a certain polynomial lies in
$\operatorname{im}(\mathcal{A}_\mathbf{u})$, which is zero under the gradient
condition.  This peels off each $r_i$ one at a time, lifting from
$\operatorname{im}(\mathcal{A}_{\mathbf{u}x_1^{2j}/\prod_{i=1}^j r_i})$ to
$\operatorname{im}(\mathcal{A}_{\mathbf{u}x_1^{2(j+1)}/\prod_{i=1}^{j+1} r_i})$.
After peeling all factors of $h$, $g x_1^k$ is coprime with $\sigma(\mathbf{u}')$,
and the hgroup lemma applies.

**Generalization note**: In the multivariate setting, complex roots of $\gcd$
factors shared with $\sigma(\mathbf{u}')$ correspond to base points on complex
varieties.  The "peeling" step replaces each such base point with a real one
(or with a generic perturbation), requiring careful analysis of the intersection
structure.

# Certificate-Based Proof

The proof has an equivalent formulation as an explicit certificate.  If for
every $p \in \Sigma[x]$ one can find $\boldsymbol{\lambda}$ and $Q \succeq 0$ such that

$$\nabla f_p(\mathbf{u})(\boldsymbol{\lambda}) + \langle Q,\, \nabla^2 f_p(\mathbf{u}) \rangle
  = -f_p(\mathbf{u}),$$

then at any SOCP (gradient $= 0$, Hessian $\succeq 0$) we immediately get
$f_p(\mathbf{u}) = 0$.

**Construction of the certificate**: Parametrize by $\eta > 0$.

1. Apply hgroup to find SOS $s$ with $p \equiv s \sigma(\mathbf{u}') \pmod{g x_1^k}$.
2. Use Bézout to find $\mathbf{b}^0$ with $2 g x_1^k \mathcal{A}_{\mathbf{u}'}(\mathbf{b}^0) = p - s\sigma(\mathbf{u}')$.
3. For each factor $r_j$ of $h$, use Bézout to find $\mathbf{b}^j$ capturing the
   correction term from the complex root $r_j$.
4. Set
   $$Q = s \bar{\mathbf{u}}' \bar{\mathbf{u}}'^{\!\top} + \tfrac{1}{\eta}\sum_j \mathbf{v}^j {\mathbf{v}^j}{}^{\!\top},
   \quad \boldsymbol{\lambda} = -(1 + \eta^{-1}\eta_{k/2+1})\mathbf{u},$$
   where $\mathbf{v}^j$ involves $g x_1^{k-2j} \bar{\mathbf{u}}' \prod_{i \le j} r_i$ and
   $\bar{\mathbf{b}}^j$, with $\eta_j = \eta^{3^j}$.

The resulting identity is

$$\nabla f_p(\mathbf{u})(\boldsymbol{\lambda}) + \langle Q, \nabla^2 f_p(\mathbf{u}) \rangle
  = -f_p(\mathbf{u}) + \sum_j \eta^{-(3^j+1)} \bigl(\langle \sigma(\mathbf{b}^j), \sigma(\mathbf{u})-p\rangle
    + 2\|\mathcal{A}_\mathbf{u}(\bar{\mathbf{b}}^j)\|^2\bigr).$$

Taking $\eta \to \infty$ sends the remainder to zero, proving $f_p(\mathbf{u}) = 0$
at any SOCP.  The certificate also extends directly to:
- **Projection onto SOS cone** (when $p \notin \Sigma$, all SOCPs have equal
  objective value = distance to $\Sigma$).
- **Nonnegativity on intervals** / weighted SOS decompositions.

# Summary of Key Tools and Their Generalization

| Tool | Univariate role | Multivariate generalization needed |
|------|-----------------|------------------------------------|
| Bézout identity / coprime kernel | $\operatorname{im}(\mathcal{A}_\mathbf{u}) = $ full space when coprime | Ideal membership / syzygies for multivariate $\mathbf{u}$; base locus of the map |
| hgroup lemma | SOS $s$ with $p \equiv sq \pmod{g}$ | SOS Positivstellensatz on a variety; requires real-algebraic geometry |
| Hermite interpolation for $\sqrt{a} \pmod{g}$ | Existence of SOS square root modulo $g$ | Local analysis at each point of the zero locus |
| Complex root peeling | Replace $h$ by $x_1^k$, iterating over complex pairs | Blowing up / deforming complex base points to real loci |
| Certificate ($\boldsymbol{\lambda}, Q$) | Explicit algebraic identity closing the proof | Same structure applies; depends on availability of the above tools |
